\section*{Part 1: Sampling-Loop Mechanics}

\subsection*{Task 1.1: The Forward Process}

The forward process adds noise per the pretrained schedule:
$x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon$,
$\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. Test image noised at
$t \in \{250, 500, 750\}$:

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.23\textwidth]{c23_0.png}\hfill
\includegraphics[width=0.23\textwidth]{c23_1.png}\hfill
\includegraphics[width=0.23\textwidth]{c23_2.png}\hfill
\includegraphics[width=0.23\textwidth]{c23_3.png}
\caption*{original / $t=250$ / $t=500$ / $t=750$}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Discussion.} As $t$ grows, $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}$ shrinks and
$\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$ grows, so the clean signal is progressively drowned out by
Gaussian noise. At $t=250$ the structure is still clearly visible; by $t=750$ the image
is almost pure noise.

\subsection*{Task 1.2a: One-Step Denoising}

Using the UNet's noise estimate to jump straight to a clean-image estimate:
$\hat x_0 = (x_t - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\hat\varepsilon_\theta(x_t, t)) / \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}$.
Null prompt \texttt{"a high quality photo"}, at $t \in \{250, 500, 750\}$:

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth]{c25_0.png}
\caption*{original}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Discussion.} At $t=250$ the one-step estimate is already a clean campanile. As $t$
rises it falls apart: at $t=500$ the tower is soft and washed out, and at $t=750$ it is a
blurry, low-contrast blob. This is expected, since the model has to reconstruct the whole clean
image from almost pure noise in a single shot, and at high noise there is not enough signal
left to do that.

\subsection*{Task 1.2b: Iterative Denoising (strided schedule)}

\texttt{strided\_timesteps = list(range(990, -1, -30))} $\rightarrow$ \textbf{34 timesteps}
(990, 960, \ldots, 30, 0), following the DDPM reverse step
\[
x_{t'} = \frac{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t'}}\,\beta_t}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\hat x_0
       + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\,(1-\bar\alpha_{t'})}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,x_t
       + \sigma_t z,
\]
with $\alpha_t = \bar\alpha_t/\bar\alpha_{t'}$, $\beta_t = 1-\alpha_t$. The iterative loop
was run starting from a test image noised to \texttt{i\_start = 10}. (The noisy
\texttt{i\_start = 10} image and the iteratively denoised result are shown side by side in
Task 1.2c below.)

\subsection*{Task 1.2c: One-Step vs.\ Iterative (same noise level, \texttt{i\_start = 10})}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\gridimg]{c31_0.png}\hfill
\includegraphics[width=\gridimg]{c31_1.png}\hfill
\includegraphics[width=\gridimg]{c31_2.png}
\caption*{noisy (i\_start=10) / iterative / one-step}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Discussion: why iterative denoising beats one-step with the same model.} The
one-step panel on the right is a smeared, ghostly campanile, while the iterative one in the
middle is a crisp tower against a clear sky. At this noise level many different clean images
could have produced the same noisy $x_t$, and the UNet only outputs a single noise estimate,
so its one-shot answer lands near the average of all of them --- which is why it looks washed
out. The iterative version removes a little noise per step; after each step the image is
slightly cleaner, fewer clean images are still consistent with it, and the model can commit
to one and start filling in real detail. The weights are identical in both runs, so the gain
comes entirely from splitting the work into many small steps rather than one big jump.

\textbf{Discussion: edit strength vs.\ \texttt{i\_start}.} \texttt{i\_start} controls how
much noise we add before denoising again, i.e.\ how far back in the schedule we jump in.
Because \texttt{strided\_timesteps} runs from $990$ down to $0$, a \emph{small}
\texttt{i\_start} corresponds to a \emph{high} timestep: at \texttt{i\_start}$=1$ we jump in
at $t\approx960$, which destroys almost all of the input, so the model has to reinvent large
chunks and produces a strong, creative edit that wanders well away from what we fed it. As
\texttt{i\_start} grows the starting timestep drops (\texttt{i\_start}$=20$ is $t\approx390$),
less noise is added, and more of the original signal survives, so by
\texttt{i\_start}$=20$ the output stays close to the input. On the campanile this shows up as
a nice progression: at \texttt{i\_start}$=7$ the tower comes back with a red-and-white
lighthouse top, at \texttt{i\_start}$=10$ it is a slimmer white lighthouse, and by
\texttt{i\_start}$=20$ it has snapped back
to essentially the original campanile. In short, \emph{small} \texttt{i\_start} gives the
strongest, most creative edit and \emph{large} \texttt{i\_start} keeps you faithful to the
original. This is clearest on the hand-drawn input, which only turns into a realistic photo
at the \emph{small}-\texttt{i\_start} settings and stays close to the drawing at
\texttt{i\_start}$=20$.

fills the masked pit region with a realistic woman's face, which is locally coherent but
clashes with the flat pixel-art style around it. So the outside stays fixed by construction,
but what gets generated inside is only as context-appropriate as the prompt allows.

\section*{Part 3: Diffusion Illusions}

\subsection*{Task 3.1: Visual Anagrams}

Combined noise estimate
$\varepsilon = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\varepsilon_\theta(x_t, t, p_1) + \text{flip}(\varepsilon_\theta(\text{flip}(x_t), t, p_2))\big)$,
$\gamma = 7$, denoised from pure noise (\texttt{i\_start = 0}).

\begin{itemize}
  \item $p_1$ (upright) $=$ \texttt{"an oil painting of an old man"}
  \item $p_2$ (flipped) $=$ \texttt{"an oil painting of people around a campfire"}
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth]{c50_0.png}\hspace{0.05\textwidth}
\includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth]{c50_1.png}
\caption*{upright: an old man / flipped: people around a campfire}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Discussion.} Averaging the two noise estimates (one on the image, one on its vertical
flip) pulls the reverse process toward both prompts under their respective orientations. In
our result the effect is uneven: the \emph{flipped} orientation reads clearly as people around
a campfire --- a bright flame low-center with orange figures around it --- while the
\emph{upright} orientation is the weaker of the two, coming out as loose oil-painting forms
with a pale blob near the top that only loosely suggests a head rather than a convincing old
man. So this sample is only a partially successful visual anagram: the campfire orientation
is clearly recognizable, but the upright old-man orientation is weak. A cleaner result would
likely need trying a few more seeds and keeping the best.

\subsection*{Task 3.2: Hybrid Images (Bonus)}

Combined estimate
$\varepsilon = f_{\text{low}}(\varepsilon_\theta(x_t, t, p_{\text{low}})) + f_{\text{high}}(\varepsilon_\theta(x_t, t, p_{\text{high}}))$,
where $f_{\text{low}}$ is a Gaussian blur (\texttt{kernel\_size=9}, \texttt{sigma=2.0}) and
$f_{\text{high}}$ is the estimate minus its own low-pass. $\gamma = 7$, \texttt{i\_start = 0}.

\begin{itemize}
  \item $p_{\text{low}}$ (low frequencies / far) $=$ \texttt{"a lithograph of waterfalls"}
  \item $p_{\text{high}}$ (high frequencies / near) $=$ \texttt{"a lithograph of a skull"}
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.35\textwidth]{c52_0.png}
\caption*{hybrid (far: waterfalls / near: skull)}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Discussion: which prompt dominates far vs.\ near, and why.} In principle the
\textbf{waterfalls} prompt should win \emph{from a distance}, since it supplies the low
spatial frequencies that set the overall shape and layout (our eyes weight low frequencies
more when an image is small or blurry), while the \textbf{skull} prompt should win \emph{up
close} through the high frequencies --- fine texture and edges we only resolve at short
range. Applying the low-pass/high-pass split to the \emph{noise estimate} rather than to the
pixels builds this separation directly into the generated image. To test the far reading we
downsampled our result to $16\times16$: a faint bright vertical form does emerge against the
dark surround and could pass for a fall, but the skull still dominates even at that size, so
the hybrid effect here is real but only partial rather than the clean two-subject switch we
were aiming for. The trick works best when the two subjects share a similar rough outline so the
low-frequency prompt can set the shape and the high-frequency prompt just adds texture on
top; a better result here would likely need different seeds or a low-frequency prompt whose
silhouette matches the skull more closely.
